TITLE: Escalation Matrix

Overview 

The escalation matrix provides a structured framework for resolving customer concerns efficiently and consistently. Its purpose is to ensure that issues are reviewed by the appropriate teams while maintaining accountability and customer satisfaction. 

A well-managed escalation process reduces resolution times, improves communication, and ensures compliance with service standards. 

 

Objectives of Escalation Management 

The escalation process aims to: 

Resolve customer issues promptly. 

Minimize repeat customer contacts. 

Improve service quality. Testtesttest

Maintain compliance. 

Provide clear ownership of customer concerns. 

Every escalation should be documented and tracked until resolution. 

 

Escalation Levels 

Level 1: Customer Service Agent 

This is the first point of contact for all customer inquiries. 

Responsibilities include: 

Policy information 

Claim status checks 

Documentation assistance 

Hospital network queries 

Benefit explanation 

The goal is to achieve first-contact resolution whenever possible. 

 

Level 2: Claims Specialist 

Cases requiring specialized knowledge are escalated to Claims Specialists. 

Examples include: 

Coverage disputes 

Medical review requirements 

Complex reimbursement issues 

Documentation discrepancies 

Settlement clarification 

Claims Specialists coordinate with hospitals, providers, and internal departments. 

 

Level 3: Senior Claims Manager 

Senior managers oversee complex and high-impact cases. 

Examples include: 

High-value claims 

Repeat escalations 

Service failures 

Exception requests 

Significant customer dissatisfaction 

Managers review decisions and provide additional oversight where necessary. 

 

Level 4: Grievance and Resolution Team 

Formal complaints and regulatory matters are escalated to the Grievance Team. 

Responsibilities include: 

Formal investigations 

Compliance reviews 

Ombudsman cases 

Regulatory complaints 

Legal notices 

These cases require strict documentation and governance. 

 

Priority Classification 

Low Priority 

Examples: 

General inquiries 

Information requests 

Portal assistance 

Resolution Goal: 

Within one business day. 

 

Medium Priority 

Examples: 

Standard claim delays 

Missing documentation 

Routine service issues 

Resolution Goal: 

Within two business days. 

 

High Priority 

Examples: 

Hospitalization issues 

Treatment authorization concerns 

Escalated complaints 

Resolution Goal: 

Within twenty-four hours. 

 

Critical Priority 

Examples: 

Emergency treatment delays 

Regulatory complaints 

Severe service failures 

Resolution Goal: 

Immediate action. 

 

Customer Communication During Escalation 

Maintaining regular communication is essential. 

Customers should receive: 

Case reference numbers 

Status updates 

Resolution timelines 

Escalation contacts 

Lack of communication can significantly increase customer dissatisfaction. 

 

Agent Responsibilities 

When escalating a case, agents should: 

Gather complete information. 

Document all customer interactions. 

Explain next steps. 

Set expectations clearly. 

Follow escalation procedures. 

 

Best Practices 

Successful escalation management includes: 

Early identification of critical cases. 

Accurate documentation. 

Timely ownership assignment. 

Proactive communication. 

Continuous follow-up. 

 

Frequently Asked Questions 

How long do escalations take? 

Resolution times depend on issue complexity and priority classification. 

Can customers track escalated cases? 

Yes. Reference numbers allow case tracking through support channels. 

What happens if no resolution is provided? 

The case may proceed to higher escalation levels, including grievance review.
